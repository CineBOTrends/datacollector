Christopher Nolan’s recent groundbreaking spectacle has cemented his status as one of the greatest filmmakers ever. Simply put, The Odyssey feels like the final inning of a perfect game. This film contains set pieces so awe-inspiring and visceral that make you feel the gravity of the story’s mythology. The horror and intrigue of the cyclops, Odysseus’ (Matt Damon) ship at the mercy of the rough sea, Odysseus’ men attacked by the giant soldiers, and the fall of Troy are all absolutely breathtaking. The sound design of the cyclops is especially stomach-churning. At the same time, the story feels grounded in Odysseus’ guilt and love for his wife Penelope (Anne Hathaway). It’s a difficult feat to accomplish, but it’s commendable in marketing this film to a mass audience and modern culture that wants to better rectify the psychological damage of war. The story utilizes signature Nolan quirks like nonlinear storytelling and exposition to slowly conduct your understanding of these characters and, at times, completely pulls the rug out from under you.

Nolan’s direction is the star of this film, but it gives each of its actors their time in the setting Sun. Each performance is very strong, but I’d expect the performances that will get the most due to be Damon’s strategic and grounded Odysseus, Hathaway’s yearning Penelope, and Robert Pattinson’s slimy Antinous. I also had a blast watching John Leguizamo’s headstrong Eumaeus, Elliot Page’s brave and tragic Sinon, Samantha Morton’s cunning and vengeful Circe, and Benny Safdie’s Agamemnon who effortlessly steals the screen with a legendary character design.

It’s difficult to not compare this film to Nolan’s Oppenheimer which came out three years earlier. Oppenheimer similarly grounds a guilt-ridden protagonist in an epic story that both requires and supersedes them. However, Oppenheimer ends with a regretful and scared J. Robert Oppenheimer as its world’s focal point and the images of nuclear weapons bringing a looming doomsday to the world. Without spoiling anything, The Odyssey feels the inverse to that ominous ending and messaging. Odysseus’ already broken character ends in an optimistic place. Where Oppenheimer shows the birth of destruction, The Odyssey shows its inevitable death. It’s a reassuring story no matter what level it’s applied!
